## AFCFTA & Cross-Border Trade Tax Considerations

The **African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)** — fully operational since 2021 — is
eliminating tariffs on 90% of goods across 54 African Union member states.

### Key implications for businesses:
- **Customs Duties**: Phase-out schedule over 5–13 years (LDCs have longer)
- **Rules of Origin**: goods must have ≥ 30% African content to qualify for preference
- **Non-Tariff Barriers**: still significant — road blocks, port delays, different standards
- **VAT/GST**: AfCFTA does NOT harmonize domestic taxes — each country still charges its own VAT
- **Transfer Pricing**: cross-border related-party transactions within Africa still require TP documentation in each country

### Practical structuring considerations:
- **Regional Headquarters**: South Africa, Mauritius, Rwanda increasingly preferred for regional holding structures
- **Mauritius Route**: Low 15% CIT, extensive DTA network (44 countries), no WHT on dividends, strong OECD BEPS compliance — popular for African investment holding companies
- **Rwanda**: 0% CGT on shares, English-speaking, strong rule of law, rising hub status
- **WAEMU zone** (West Africa): Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Mali etc. share CFA franc and harmonized OHADA business law — easier multi-country operations

## OHADA Business Law (17 West/Central African Countries)

**Organisation for the Harmonisation of Business Law in Africa**
Countries: Benin, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, CAR, Chad, Comoros, Congo, DRC, Côte d'Ivoire, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, Mali, Niger, Senegal, Togo

Key features:
- **SYSCOHADA** accounting system: the required accounting framework (like IFRS for the OHADA zone)
- Uniform Business Act: governs company formation, contracts, securities, debt recovery
- CCJA (court): common arbitration and appeals court for commercial disputes
- **UEMOA VAT harmonization**: standard rate is 18% across WAEMU states

## Transfer Pricing in Africa

Many African countries now have TP regulations (Nigeria 2018, Kenya 2006, South Africa 1995,
Ghana 2020, Rwanda 2014, Uganda 2011, Tanzania 2014).

### Arm's Length Principle:
Transactions between related parties must be priced as if between independent parties.

### Required documentation:
- **Master File**: group-level info on business, TP policies, intercompany transactions
- **Local File**: country-specific details on material transactions
- **Country-by-Country Report (CbCR)**: for groups with revenue > USD 750M equivalent

### Acceptable methods (OECD, adopted by most African countries):
1. Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP)
2. Resale Price Method (RPM)
3. Cost Plus Method
4. Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM)
5. Profit Split Method
