# Financial Statements Reference Guide

## The Three Core Statements

These three statements are interconnected. Changes in one ripple through the others.

```
INCOME STATEMENT (P&L)          BALANCE SHEET               CASH FLOW STATEMENT
─────────────────────────────  ──────────────────────────  ──────────────────────
Revenue                         ASSETS                       Operating Activities
- Cost of Sales                   Current Assets             Investing Activities
= Gross Profit                    Non-Current Assets         Financing Activities
- Operating Expenses            LIABILITIES                  ─────────────────────
= EBIT                            Current Liabilities        Net Change in Cash
- Interest                        Non-Current Liabilities
= EBT                           EQUITY
- Tax                             Share Capital
= Net Profit                      Retained Earnings
      │                                  ▲
      └──────────Net Profit added to Retained Earnings each year──────────────────
```

## Notes to Financial Statements

Every set of financial statements must include notes:

1. **Accounting policies**: Basis of preparation, revenue recognition, depreciation method, inventory valuation, foreign currency
2. **Fixed asset schedule**: Movements for the year (additions, disposals, depreciation)
3. **Borrowings**: Terms, interest rates, security, repayment schedule
4. **Related party transactions**: Loans from directors, sales to connected companies
5. **Contingent liabilities**: Pending lawsuits, guarantees
6. **Events after reporting date**: Significant events between year-end and signing date
7. **Staff costs**: Average headcount; total wages/salaries/pension
8. **Tax**: Deferred tax calculations; effective tax rate reconciliation

## Fixed Asset Schedule (Depreciation Rates)

### Common Useful Lives & Methods

| Asset Category | Useful Life | Method | Annual Rate |
|---------------|-------------|--------|-------------|
| Buildings | 40–50 years | Straight-line | 2–2.5% |
| Leasehold improvements | Lease term | Straight-line | Over lease |
| Plant & machinery | 10–20 years | Reducing balance | 10–20% |
| Motor vehicles | 4–5 years | Reducing balance | 25–33% |
| Computers & IT equipment | 3–5 years | Straight-line | 20–33% |
| Furniture & fittings | 8–10 years | Straight-line | 10–12.5% |
| Software (purchased) | 3–5 years | Straight-line | 20–33% |

### Nigeria-Specific (Capital Allowances — CITA)
FIRS allows capital allowances (not accounting depreciation) for tax purposes:

| Asset | Initial Allowance | Annual Allowance |
|-------|------------------|-----------------|
| Industrial plant | 50% | 25% |
| Agricultural plant | 95% | — |
| Non-industrial buildings | 15% | 10% |
| Motor vehicles | 50% | 25% |
| Plantation equipment | 95% | — |
| Mining | 95% | — |

*Deferred tax arises from the difference between accounting depreciation and tax capital allowances.*

## IFRS vs Local GAAP Key Differences

| Topic | IFRS | Nigerian SAS / GAAS |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| Revenue recognition | IFRS 15 — 5-step model | Simpler accrual rules |
| Leases | IFRS 16 — most leases on balance sheet | Operating leases off balance sheet |
| Financial instruments | IFRS 9 — expected credit losses | Incurred loss model |
| Biological assets | IAS 41 — fair value | Cost model |
| Hyperinflation | IAS 29 | Not specifically addressed |

**IFRS adoption in Africa**:
- Nigeria: IFRS mandatory for public interest entities (banks, insurance, listed companies); IFRS for SMEs encouraged for others
- Ghana, Kenya, South Africa: Full IFRS adoption for PIEs; IFRS for SMEs for others
- Many smaller businesses use local accounting standards or simplified rules
